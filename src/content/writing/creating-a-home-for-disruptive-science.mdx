## Institutions as technologies [[TOC: Introduction]]

UK science policy is greatly concerned with platform technologies – artificial intelligence, quantum, synthetic biology – as if the crucial decisions are about bits, qubits and base pairs. But the most foundational platform, the one that determines whether such technologies are invented and developed in Britain at all, is institutional.

Institutions are technologies in their own right: architectures with design choices, affordances and constraints. The organisation of laboratories and funding, the incentives hardwired into the system, the timescales optimised for each promotes certain types of research while rendering others near impossible.

For decades, the UK has run its publicly funded science mainly on one platform: the university. Britain’s universities are world-class, but no institutional structure is well suited to all modes of research.

The standard academic operating system – short-term grant cycles, publish-or-perish culture, heavy teaching loads and assessment tied to specific blanket metrics – cannot run certain types of research programmes.

## Incubating a revolution

In particular, the current academia-focused landscape struggles to support scientific missions that are high-risk or run counter to mainstream opinion, require tight coupling between discovery and invention, and call for continuity of people and infrastructure over decades. Work where theorists, experimentalists and engineers must iterate side by side, chasing ideas too heretical for consensus funding, too long-term to survive on a patchwork of grants and too speculative for imminent commercialisation.

Historically, labs that could support this kind of work have pioneered new scientific domains, industrial ecosystems and human capabilities.

Bell Labs and Xerox Parc in the US and the Laboratory of Molecular Biology in Cambridge were unconventional spaces that plucked nascent possibilities from the ether and solidified them into concrete advancements, incubating revolutions in telecommunications, personal computing and molecular biology, respectively. Their particular structures enabled a kind of science that simply could not happen elsewhere.

Britain should learn from these transformative precedents and push further, designing and building a contemporary home for this type of research.

## Upending tradition

To this end, our recent report from the Tony Blair Institute proposes a national [network of Lovelace Disruptive Invention Labs](https://institute.global/insights/tech-and-digitalisation/a-new-national-purpose-reimagining-uk-science-and-technology-through-lovelace-disruptive-invention-laboratories), places where small, fluid and cross-disciplinary teams can pursue ambitious bets over many years.

Full core funding would release researchers from the insecurity of working grant to grant and the pressure of publishing, allowing them to chase more distant horizons and pursue the unconventional.

Perhaps most critically, these labs would invert the traditional career model by trusting promising early career researchers with resources and independence, prioritising hands-on research over empire-building and agency over hierarchy.

If this approach sounds reminiscent of the UK’s Advanced Research and Invention Agency, that’s no accident. Aria was created to fund high-risk, high-reward exploratory programmes with long timelines and operational flexibility.

But a funding agency, however bold, is not set up to establish and sustain a network of physical labs at the scale of our proposals. Lovelace Labs are a natural complement: new institutional hardware on which new programmes of science can run. Without such institutions, we leave a gap between brilliant bets and the spaces where those bets can mature into new fields.

## Extend the UK’s lead

The modern-day workshops for institutional experimentation are often the labs of tech giants – Google DeepMind is a prime example – where monopoly-scale revenues create room for ambition and stability that competitive pressures would otherwise preclude, as with AT&T and Xerox before them.

US philanthropists are also backing new research organisations that implement much of the thinking in our report. The four-year-old [Arc Institute](https://arcinstitute.org/about) has already delivered impressive advances. Others include [Future House](https://www.futurehouse.org/) and [Arcadia](https://www.arcadiascience.com/).

Those with the most resources and options are choosing to upgrade institutional technology: just last week, Sam Altman, chief executive of OpenAI, announced his support for [Episteme](https://epistemescience.substack.com/p/introducing-episteme), a new research organisation designed to allow “exceptional scientists to pursue high-impact ideas that have languished in traditional institutions”.

Public science should not be left behind; it even has natural advantages in this area. Governments are well placed to make long-horizon portfolio bets, taking a broad view of value that includes spillovers, regional clusters, new capabilities and anchor institutions for industries that do not yet exist.

Britain, in establishing Aria, is so far the only country putting serious public money behind institutional experimentation at scale. It enjoys a lead we should extend.

The Royal Society, the university, the US Advanced Research Projects Agency – each began as a bold institutional experiment then went on to change the trajectory of scientific possibility. In the 21st century, as science grows more complex, global competition for talent intensifies and the age of big data and AI dawns, we once again need to reimagine our pursuit of the future. Lovelace Labs should be one experiment in a broader shift towards a research ecosystem that matches institutional structure to purpose.

Change the operating system, and you change what programmes can run. The next great British invention might not be a technology at all, but rather a new way of making technologies possible.
