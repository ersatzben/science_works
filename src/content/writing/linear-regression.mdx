## Summary

### The argument

* The UK government's innovation narrative relies on a single picture: the **linear model of innovation**, an imagined one-way pipeline from new ideas through new technologies to economic value.

* The model is legible and politically attractive, but wrong. It misunderstands **where innovation comes from** and **how innovation creates national wealth**. A policy programme built on it will deliver neither breakthroughs nor growth.

### Section A: Where Innovation Comes From

* The linear model misses critical ways innovation happens, so **whole** **categories of productive scientific work go under-recognised** **and under-supported**, impoverishing the scientific commons.

* The model also misreads where value lies: **returns to research are heavily concentrated in a small number of breakthroughs.** A system that maximises reliable measurable throughput (papers, patents, spin-outs) filters out the science that can produce exceptional success.

* ***Prescription***: support the scientific commons that breakthroughs grow from – shared infrastructure, instruments, datasets – and look for the highest ceiling, not the safest floor.

### Section B: How Innovation Creates Economic Value

* The linear model **prioritises one route to value: scaling British, VC-backed, IP-based firms.** If the state perceives that too few companies are progressing down that path, it assumes that there is a problem for it to fix.

* **Any bespoke support should be justified case-by-case or for strategic reasons, not economic ones.** The linear model tempts the state to help promising companies over hurdles and to pick winners. But firm failure is how the system learns and reallocates; lists of champions create can't-fail firms and moral hazard; and governments cannot beat the market at selection anyway.

* **Success comes in many forms.** The narrow linear model misses the other ways innovation creates value: acquisitions, licensing, and IP agreements; and revenue-led businesses outside deeptechs. It also fixates the state on ownership when what enriches a country is activity – research, manufacturing, jobs on its soil – not shareholder nationality.

* **Keeping activity here means making Britain attractive,** by fixing energy costs, talent access, and trade barriers, and by structuring firm-specific support such that it benefits Britain, while investing in assets that anchor firms. These approaches can also attract foreign firms’ activity.

* ***Prescription:*** improve the conditions every company faces rather than picking champions; anchor activity with hard-to-move assets and support with strings attached; and court foreign firms' activity as keenly as we keep our own.

### Section C: Why The Model Persists

* It is **legible**, and can explain innovation to a non-expert in seconds.

* It is **politically convenient**, amplified by the incumbents (universities, VCs) who profit from pipeline funding.

* It offers the **illusion of control**, focusing on visible, attributable wins within a single parliament.

## The Long Game

You have been given total control over the government’s tech strategy. You have a mandate from the PM and a budget which, while small compared to frontier labs’, is nothing to be sniffed at. How will you spend your capital, time, and effort? 

Your first option offers rapid results and recognition. Look around the country, count the world-beating tech companies, and decide that the number is too low. Identify the most promising start-ups and back them, doubling down where we’re strong. Ask them what they need and give it to them: capital, contracts, a concierge. Help them over hurdles, pick them up when they fall. Bathe in the reflected glow of their funding rounds, each larger than the last. Rear a generation of unicorns; carve out your own Silicon Valley. If the companies go abroad, at least you did your best. And, because you don’t forget the future, double the number of PhD students researching AI.

Another approach is slower and less glamorous. Build the things that scale-ups need and which anchor them here. Lay a statutory instrument allowing them to build out compute; work with Ofgem to allow privately-funded grid connections; put guardrails around public data assets so they can be released; tweak visa eligibility; limit procurements to British companies. Don’t prespecify who will benefit – these companies may not even exist yet. Weather the storm when you reject firms’ calls for unrestricted grants and clawback-free equity. Remove or lower the hurdles on the racetrack, but don’t pick in advance which racers must reach the finish line. Resign yourself to criticism from firms that leave (of course they *would* have stayed if the government had just provided that grant), and firms that stay, who need *just a little* more equity to achieve their potential. Don’t expect any credit when firms win. Also, double the number of AI PhD students.

The first option is more attractive. The second approach works.

## The Linear Model of Innovation

In recent months, the UK government has pushed innovation policy up its agenda. Treasury ministers [have](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/entrepreneurship-in-the-uk) [asked](https://www.gov.uk/government/speeches/mais-lecture-2026) businesses to “start, scale, and stay” in the country; public R\&D investment is being [overhauled](https://www.ukri.org/publications/explainer-ukri-budget-allocations/budget-allocations-for-uk-research-and-innovation/) into three “buckets” of spend, separating basic research, priority applications, and business support; and the UK’s innovation agency is [pivoting](https://www.ukri.org/publications/turning-breakthrough-ideas-into-the-uks-next-industry-giants/innovate-uk-turning-breakthrough-ideas-into-industry-giants/) its support towards scaling companies “to enable breakthrough ideas to become industry giants”. 

The government is right to focus on how it can create prosperity from ideas. Innovation is the closest thing economics has to a free lunch, because it can [generate](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/economic-sciences/2025/summary/) sustained [growth](https://www.journals.uchicago.edu/doi/abs/10.1086/738899) – the most effective tonic to the nation’s ills. A nation that harnesses the power of innovation is buying a part of the future, and a higher likelihood of having rich citizens, solvent public services, and strong defence.

There’s lots we could do to help us claim that free lunch. We could build shared infrastructure that firms cluster around and cannot easily detach themselves from, as Flanders has done in Ghent, where the open-access [Bio Base Europe Pilot Plant](https://www.bbeu.org/) gives biotech firms fermentation capacity none could afford to build alone. We could support the dynamic flow of people and applied knowledge, as Germany does through its network of [Fraunhofer institutes](https://www.fraunhofer.de/en/about-fraunhofer.html). We could import, adapt, and adopt ideas and technology developed elsewhere, as both China and the US have done in their own very different ways. There are many approaches besides, and the right strategy takes the best of each. Indeed, one can point to areas where the UK is already trying elements of several.

But notice what the government's headline approaches – "Start, scale, stay"; the buckets; Innovate UK’s promise to "pull the strongest ideas from across the UK through to commercialisation" – have in common. All three are predicated on a single, shared picture of how innovation creates value.

This is the **linear model of innovation**: an imagined one-way pipeline from new ideas to economic value via new technologies. When applied to technology development (we’ll call this Linear A), the model holds that new ideas are produced by universities and applied by businesses as they develop products, with consumers then adopting the technology and benefiting from improved productivity or better lives. When applied to company growth (Linear B), it holds that firms form around new ideas at the frontier of human knowledge, grow via investing venture capital in an initially loss-making product, and turn profitable once they sell enough and have finished the hardest parts of development. Successful companies are rewarded with a hefty market listing, the economy benefits from high-wage jobs, and the exchequer benefits from increased tax. In both versions of the model, the state's implied role is the same: fund the upstream research, derisk the investment in application and development, and reward downstream success with light-touch regulation, low taxes, and procurement contracts.

<Figure src="/assets/images/writing/linear-regression/01.png" />

The linear model has been scholars' favourite whipping boy for so long that some [question](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/313772710_%27The_Linear_Model%27_Did_Not_Exist_Reflections_on_the_History_and_Historiography_of_Science_and_Research_in_Industry_in_the_Twentieth_Century) whether anyone has ever sincerely believed in it. Despite this, the linear model has outsized sway over how the government spends, strategises, and directs its attention. Money for universities to produce new ideas; more money to help businesses commercialise them; and increasing subsidies to coax venture capital into scaling companies: funding at every stage of the pipeline. 

The model is superficially appealing. It gives the government a simple story with an unambiguously positive goal and clear staging posts. It gives universities a case for more funding at the top of the pipeline, and it gives investors a case for more public capital further down. 

A more nuanced account of how innovation works and how it creates value risks muddying the water and weakening the case for investment altogether. It may even be that some such simplification is the only way to get government to care about innovation at all, especially if ministers are more at home with the old-world economy of factories and trains. 

But the linear model’s beauty is only skin deep. The fact that it is so enticing makes it all the more important to understand where it falls short, and fall short it does.

We argue that an over-reliance on linear thinking hamstrings us twice over: in making technological breakthroughs and in reaping the economic bounty that innovation can bring. The model fails on both counts for the same underlying reasons. It is too narrow, because it can see only one route from idea to value, and so excludes the many other ways that research creates knowledge and that innovation creates wealth. And it is too afraid of failure, because its logic is so sequential: wherever the pipeline narrows, leaving ideas unapplied or firms not scaling, the model diagnoses a blockage for the state to clear. 

These tendencies shape the culture of our innovation economy. One kind of company – the VC-backed British scale-up in a prestige industry – is prized above all others, including revenue-led businesses, overseas companies, and firms outside deeptech. It becomes tempting to use lists or frameworks for picking winners: naming sovereign champions, buying stakes in fashionable AI firms, and fretting over the location of owners rather than the location of laboratories and jobs. Promising ideas are dragged bodily down the pipeline, out of the environments that produced them, through a spin-out, towards a listing, whether that journey is ultimately good for the country or not.

## Where Innovation Comes From

New ideas are often necessary and prior to new technologies. If you don’t know the structure of DNA, you don’t get DNA tests for criminal cases; no relativity, no satellite navigation. Discovery does sometimes come first and make invention possible, just as the linear model says. 

But the model misses critical features of how innovation plays out in practice. James Watt was building steam engines decades before we understood the laws of thermodynamics; Malcom McLean revolutionised global trade by recombining existing elements and processes to develop the shipping container – without relying on newly developed theory; and even the discovery of the structure of DNA was made possible only thanks to a new technique – Franklin’s hydration-controlled fibre preparation – not a new idea.

Innovation, in practice, is often messy. It moves not down a straight line, but through complex loops of feedback and iteration; pulled by market forces, shaped by organisational learning, and driven as often by new methods and tools as by new theories. The linear model is so reductive that it is misleading and not a good guide to action. 

The model treats innovation as a staged, attributable output to be maximised, when in reality it is a recursive search across a landscape where value is not evenly distributed, which causes damage to scientific systems in two ways. First, it cannot see whole categories of how innovation occurs, so a system built on it under-resources (or suppresses) them. Second, it misreads where value lies. Returns to research are not spread evenly but concentrated in a tail. A model predicated on volume moving stage to stage has no way to reckon with this, and a system that maximises throughput will drift towards conservatism, low variance, and risk aversion – a natural consequence of which is that high-variance science does not get the precedence it deserves. We consider each of these problems in turn to make the case that a system built on linear assumptions cannot deliver the disruptive innovation it claims to desire.

## A road too narrow for disruption [[TOC: – A road too narrow]]

The theoretical problems with the linear model have been known for decades, but they bear restating. In short, it fails to recognise several important features of innovation:

* **Theory and application advance together.** In many of the most innovative spaces, discovery and invention are knotted [together](https://www.nber.org/system/files/chapters/c2141/c2141.pdf), each informing the other, as the case of the [transistor](https://www.nber.org/system/files/chapters/c2141/c2141.pdf) famously shows. Attempts to build products produce knowledge, and theoretical understanding is sharpened on the whetstone of reality. The linear model has no way to represent that mutual dependence, as it runs in only one direction, separating the production of knowledge from its uses.

* **Tools are often upstream, not downstream.** New instruments can create new science. Looking only at  biology, X-ray crystallography, gene sequencing, mass spectrometry, cryo-EM, and now AlphaFold each opened avenues of discovery that did not exist before. A model that puts "ideas" at the source and tools and technologies as downstream “applied” products will be flowing backwards half the time.

* **Demand, not just discovery, drives new products.** The model's single direction runs from new knowledge onward: science produces ideas; applications or products follow. But a great deal of valuable innovation begins at the other end, when an unmet need pulls existing capability into a new shape with no frontier discovery required. It was unmet needs, not new ideas, that drove the development of the wheeled suitcase or the bicycle. The wheel had been around for several millennia before anyone attached it to a bag and mass produced it. The novelty was the much broader demand for convenient travel storage, which the linear model misses.

* **Much innovation is recombination: the connective tissue matters as much as the frontier.** New technologies are [overwhelmingly](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0048733315000840) [fresh](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/rsif/article/12/106/20150272/35717/Invention-as-a-combinatorial-process-evidence-from) [combinations](https://www.amazon.co.uk/Nature-Technology-What-How-Evolves/dp/0141031638) of components that already [exist](https://stianstian.medium.com/darpa-vs-the-wheely-suitcase-d4ffc0a05f3a), not applications of newly discovered knowledge. Consider once more the wheeled suitcase: the constituent parts were already widespread. The innovation was the combination. Since innovation is in many cases recombination, the *accessible stock* of combinable components matters as much as the flow of frontier ideas – which [argues](https://ideas.repec.org/a/oup/qjecon/v113y1998i2p331-360..html) for diffusion, interoperability, standards, accessible infrastructure, and open tools. These are precisely the hard-to-attribute activity the linear model deprioritises in favour of novelty at the frontier.

These theoretical problems are practically important. A model that fundamentally misunderstands how innovation happens in reality is a bad guide to making breakthroughs happen. Each of these mechanisms of innovation –  loops, tools, demand, recombination – is invisible to the model and will be under-resourced by a system built on it.

Worse, there is little way to correct this within a linear-reliant system. You can only change what you can measure, and a linear-reliant system will measure the success metrics that matter for linear innovation – papers, patents, spin-outs. It won’t even count other things that matter, like the number and type of tools being built, the methods being developed, or the stock of knowledge. The scientific commons are so neglected that it is hard even to quantify the problem.

This is particularly harmful because the environments with the greatest innovative capacity have tended to be conspicuously non-linear. Breakthroughs cluster where disciplines collide and where theory, tools and engineering [work](https://www.hup.harvard.edu/books/9780674967960) [together](https://www.hup.harvard.edu/books/9780674251854), often with market forces. Many of the most important inventions of the past century have come from just such environments – personal computing at Xerox PARC, the transistor at Bell Labs, the transformer behind today’s LLMs at Google, DNA sequencing at the Laboratory of Molecular Biology.

Nor is this non-linearity restricted only to the exploratory, frontier-pushing laboratories of legend. Dense patterns of invention show up around industrial sites, large shared instruments, and long-running dataset programmes: spaces organised not as stages in linear pipelines but as hubs for many different kinds of knowledge and technology creation. Proximity, collision, and feedback are essential, not incidental, for productive cycles of discovery and invention. 

If the UK is to maximise its scientific potential, its research and funding institutions need to account for these non-linear features. Instead, Britain routes an unusual amount of its discovery and invention work through a single source: universities. Modern British universities are often highly linear settings. Research is organised into academic departments that inherit the boundaries of the disciplines they house. In practice, many university labs stick broadly within the constructed constraints of the basic/applied distinction, rarely moving fluidly across it – with little integration of engineers with scientists and technicians. 

The UK’s linear approach to commercialising research compounds this by removing promising ideas from environments where they can thrive. As university research tends to stick within particular boundaries, a new idea often has to leave when it is commercialised. Once a piece of research shows applied promise, the instinct is to move it – and often a talented researcher or two – out of the lab and start it on its one-way journey to be developed elsewhere. A new spark is removed from the environment in which it was formed, away from the tinder that could catch and grow into a blaze. What the model promotes – knowledge and talent handed off as rapidly as possible from one stage to the next – can sometimes mark a premature end of an exchange that may have produced much more. Spinning out is often the right way to maximise the value from individual ideas, but if it is seen as the *only* way to develop an idea, we won’t  get maximum value from the research programme as a whole. Thus, the linear model limits the creation of technoscientific revolutions by tempting us to dismantle the environments that can produce them. 

The UK's unusual over-reliance on academia, spin-outs, and on siloed funding, contributes significantly to, and is partly a consequence of, linear thinking. As [others](https://worksinprogress.co/issue/innovation-is-not-linear/) have noted, the way we fund and organise our scientific endeavours needs to consider innovation as it actually is – loops, demand, and all. 

## Focusing on the quantity of innovation reduces total returns [[TOC: – Quality not quantity]]

The most common account of Britain's scientific and technological ills runs like this: we are a discovery science superpower – four of the world's top ten universities, a perennial high finish in the Global Innovation Index, the highest field-weighted citation impact of competitor nations – but we fail to convert this into an economic advantage. Too much good research falls into a "valley of death" between the lab and the point at which a technology is investable; tech transfer offices make spinning out slow, costly and unattractive. Many argue that if we can fix the translation layer – more spin-outs, more proof-of-concept funding, lower university equity stakes – the science will convert into technologies, therapies, and growth. In the words of Innovate UK: 

> The UK has one of the strongest research bases in the world. But we do not have as many globally scaled companies as that strength should produce… The UK excels at making discoveries, generating intellectual property (IP), spinning out and starting up. Yet when it comes to scaling, too many innovative businesses fail, stall or move overseas.

Some of this narrative is true. The UK is very strong in many areas of discovery science; high university equity stakes have been a problem; more patient capital would help. But this  is not the whole story.

The linear model overfocuses on pushing ideas through the pipeline because its theory of technology development is so strongly sequential. The health of the innovation system is measured by how much quantified innovation passes through each potential bottleneck. If value flows in one direction, from research at the top to applications at the bottom, then you can get more technological development by pushing more in at the top (more PhDs, higher research intensity, more papers) or at strategic points along the way (more funding for TTOs, lower equity stakes). Each potentially applicable idea must be dragged bodily down the pipeline. There is a clear desire for high measured throughput and if any metric, whether citations or spin-outs, is not being maximised, that is seen as a problem to be solved. This is partly why many believe there is a problem with the number of spin-outs, despite us spinning out firms at the same [rate](https://www.ifm.eng.cam.ac.uk/research/uci-policy-unit/uci-news/spinning-out-success/) as the US.  

This focus on the quantity of innovation distracts us from the important question of quality, trapping public debate in a resource war pattern. More funding is almost always seen as the answer – the question is too often “how much?” rather than “how?”. The debate about whether we have enough spin-outs stops us asking when it is best for a university TTO to lead on commercialisation and when that task is better left to venture builders like Science Creates. As Adam Glen has [argued](https://adamglen.substack.com/p/we-fund-the-system-not-the-founders), “TTOs are \[…\] not asked to show whether founders succeeded, whether technologies reached patients, or whether the public got a return on its investment. So \[...\] over time, they stop thinking about them.” The quality question is crowded out by the quantity question. The same goes for citations, papers, and more. More money for basic research and more money for applied research will, without other changes, deliver more of the same. 

This relegation of quality is particularly problematic because the returns on innovation are dominated by the winners. The quality of the best, most disruptive innovation overwhelmingly [determines](https://www.experimental-history.com/p/science-is-a-strong-link-problem) the output of the overall innovation system. On any metric you look at, from [citations](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1002/asi.21448) to patents and [scale-ups](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S004873339900089X) to [scientists](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2012208118) themselves, a vast corpus of evidence tells us that a small fraction of work produces most of the impact. In technical terms, the distribution of returns to research is right-skewed.

In this context, a focus on quantity is self-defeating. Volume is the wrong unit when returns are skewed. Optimising the count actively works against capturing the tail, because the systems that maximise reliable average output – steady publication, predictable milestones, consensus peer review – are exactly the ones that filter out high-variance science. Quantity-fixation and risk-aversion are often intimately related. To fixate on volume is to suppress variance, and a system built to keep the pipeline full tends to become, intentionally or otherwise, a system built to minimise risk and miss the tail.

The linear model is not solely responsible for our R\&D ecosystem’s systemic risk-aversion, and public money should not be poured into work that will never yield anything. But the narrow conception of yield and the quantity-focused culture that result from overly linear thinking are a significant part of the problem. The research funding system should aim to support science with the highest ceiling, rather than the highest average return or, worse, the highest floor. Everyone in research funding recognises this, but the UK funding system overall still relies too heavily on modes of research funding that disadvantage [novel](https://www.science.org/content/article/funding-agency-s-reviewers-were-biased-against-scientists-novel-ideas), [risky](https://www.nber.org/papers/w33495), or [interdisciplinary](https://www.nature.com/articles/nature18315) ideas relative to [safer](https://www.newthingsunderthesun.com/pub/rogqzrma/release/17) [bets](https://mattsclancy.substack.com/p/biases-against-risky-research). Grant agencies are looking at this, and the work of ARIA and the UK [Metascience Unit](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/a-year-in-metascience-2025) is particularly encouraging, but they are fighting an uphill battle against the prevailing assumption that the quantity of science is the most important factor. The linear logic of the system setup is hard to escape.

## Taking the straitjacket off innovation [[TOC: – Taking the straightjacket off]]

The linear model cannot see how innovation happens, so it fails to support important types  of innovative activity. This is a poor blueprint to organise a research system around. Organising and funding activity based on a spurious distinction between basic & applied research, and tracking progress as moving down this line, with the attendant fear of failure, actively forbids the feedback loops that are so important in innovation. Because the linear model fails to see these loops and distracts from the fact that some innovation is much more valuable than the rest, policy agendas that embed linear distinctions into the funding architecture are hindered.

What would it mean to fund and build differently? If breakthroughs come from collision, recombination, and the feedback between use and understanding, we should fund the ground on which those happen: shared infrastructure, instruments, datasets, the technical skill to build and run them – the commons that many kinds of researcher gather around. We should also fund for the highest ceiling, not the safest floor, making explicit bets on research that some people think could change the world, even if many others disagree. This takes bravery and a strong risk appetite, but funders who show that courage will be handsomely repaid.

## How Innovation Creates Economic Value [[TOC: How Innovation Creates Value]]

Businesses, of course, famously spend as much time as possible reading government missives. But for those few that haven’t had the time, a summary: there has been a clear throughline in the government’s economic interventions over the last year. The [Industrial Strategy](https://assets.publishing.service.gov.uk/media/69256e16367485ea116a56de/industrial_strategy_policy_paper.pdf) asked companies to “start, scale, list, and stay here”. At the Budget, the Treasury repeated the call to “start, scale and stay” in a [document](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/entrepreneurship-in-the-uk) directly aimed at entrepreneurs. The theme returned at the [Mais Lecture](https://www.gov.uk/government/speeches/mais-lecture-2026), alongside an intention to foster “strategic national champions”. Each time, the government wheeled out the obligatory references to British universities leading the world and to “vibrant” start-up systems, as measured by start-ups created or VC raised. The linear model of economic innovation is simple and easy to get behind: professors produce ideas, then spin-outs develop tech, scale with VC cash, and boost economic growth. The conceit is that scaling innovative businesses reallocates resources to more productive firms, which compete for employees, drive up wages, and bolster the exchequer with their taxed profits. The government’s economic rhetoric is the linear model in its purest firm-focused form.

Fostering the next-generation of world-beating companies in a winner-takes-most competition is an admirable goal. If the UK can do so, it will be the richer for it. The tech boom in Silicon Valley has seen California’s economy become larger than almost any country’s. Innovative companies occupy a position high up the value chain, with plenty of value added. As the world becomes more competitive, it becomes even more important to  ensure that the UK is home to some of those winners. The UK has for too long aimed to be merely a science superpower, winning academia’s [glass bead game](https://www.amazon.co.uk/Glass-Bead-Game-Vintage-Classics/dp/009928362X) by maximising our scores on field-weighted citation impact, H-index, and university rankings. But this is not good enough unless it secures broad and deep economic benefits for the country. It is right to focus on getting a return on our scientific strengths, and one plausible way to do that is to become home to large innovative companies.

This goal is so obviously attractive that it is implicitly shared by governments around the world. But very few have managed to replicate California’s success. We like to think that, if anyone can succeed, Britain could. But over-relying on this model – and betting the UK’s economic future on your ability to mimic Silicon Valley – is a big risk, and is not the only strategy available to us. We used to pursue a more diverse range of approaches in our innovation policy. Before Thatcher, the government was much more [active](https://uclpress.co.uk/book/science-policy-under-thatcher/%20) in directly funding civil technology efforts. The success of that approach is [debateable](https://www.bsg.ox.ac.uk/sites/default/files/2023-12/BSG-WP%E2%80%932023-056-When-Missions-Fail.pdf), but it is clear that putting scaling innovative companies at the heart of your innovation strategy, while often the default, is not inevitable. In this section we detail how overly linear thinking pathologises firm failure and narrows policymakers’ understanding of which companies and types of innovation can bring economic, industrial, and social benefits. 

## Supporting scaling companies does not mean helping each one grow at all costs [[TOC: – No support at all costs]]

The linear model thinks that economic value comes at the end of a series of steps. Moving to the next step is, in this model, a clearly good and necessary thing. A scale-up is closer to a huge British business than a start-up is, so if the government sees a promising spin-out struggling to raise capital, it should step in to help it over the hurdles that stand in the way of the finish line. This is obvious, intuitive, and wrong. 

### Firm failure can be system success

In fact, many companies need to fail for the system to thrive. Scale-up is a high-risk time: failure is common as businesses are exposed to the discipline of the market and forced to prove that their product has a place. The failure of a business is valuable information about what works and what doesn’t, and it is actively undesirable for all businesses to scale. When a plausible business fails to become profitable, the scarce resources it is using – talent, expertise, prime office space – are freed up, becoming available to companies that the market has deemed more competitive. Failure is bad for the firm but good for the system. There is, therefore, a clear difference between system success and firm success. While the linear model clearly charts the path many of the eventually successful businesses will have taken, it does not imply that the system would be better if all businesses followed as far along that same path as they can, still less that the government should help them do so. The best tech companies are highly productive because they have been selected and trained by consistent competitive pressure. Removing that pressure will not achieve the government’s goals and could even stifle competition and innovation. If a company requires government support at each stage – being ushered by various quangos or a [concierge](https://x.com/peterkyle/status/2064249699947696334) along an integrated pathway, from Innovate UK to the British Business Bank to the National Wealth Fund – that is a strong signal that it is not competitive.

### Picking the next Apple in the Garden of the Hesperides 

It is tempting to think that we can get around the problem of firm failure being desirable by simply identifying which companies are important and limiting bespoke state help to this inner circle. The idea is that Britain’s scaling companies are in an international race and need to be pushed as quickly as possible towards the finish line. Since the state has limited funding and organisational resources, it is good to target these towards companies that look like plausible global winners. Doing so also allows firm failure outside of these national champions. In theory, this provides the best of both worlds: competition and firm failure is preserved, but the government can still double down where the UK is strong and help those special companies avoid failure or stagnation. The magnetism of this idea extends beyond officials in an interventionist state, and there have also been [calls](https://articape.substack.com/p/act-4-max-q-and-the-modern-beads) for such a preferential list by successful entrepreneurs and investors. It seems like low-hanging fruit: just pick the next Apples.

But picking low-hanging fruit is not always easy. Heracles found the apples of the Hesperides to be unpickable by mortal hands, Adam and Eve discovered that it is not always wise to try, and that is to say nothing of Tantalus’ experience. Our ability to pick the companies of the future is similarly mythical. At best, it will solve problems only for a few selected businesses, leaving others facing a competitive disadvantage, while distracting from the broader economic reforms we need. At worst, it will create a new generation of state-backed incumbents, allowing inefficient businesses to stumble on regardless, blocking opportunities for new disruptors, and paving the way for ever more dirigisme. 

The most pernicious version of state selection would be a register of allegedly strategic companies. If the government were to name, whether publicly or privately, a list of champions, it would face pressure from the named champions to support them come what may. The list would create “can’t-fail firms”, with all the costly moral hazard that entails. If a chosen champion were going under, they would call for a bailout. If they were being outcompeted – or simply not dominating the market – the temptation to “unlock their economic potential” with just one more subsidy would be hard to resist.

Any such list would pick the wrong companies. No government will find a reliable way to beat the market, and if any were to come close, it would not be the one that took more than a year to write down its industrial strategy. Even if the list is miraculously right at first, it will swiftly be rendered obsolete by the dynamic nature of innovation. It might initially seem that this obsolescence problem could be fixed with a more dynamic framework, which selects companies for preferential treatment according to whether they meet certain criteria. But the approach remains doomed. Companies will contort themselves to meet the framework’s conditions, distracting their leadership from economic activity. Once a company comfortably meets the framework’s thresholds, it will still become a can’t-fail firm, with all the attendant moral hazard. Moreover, to manage this risk, such a framework would still need to reliably pick out early-stage future winners with a low rate of failure, something that few if any investors have ever managed to do. So, neither a list nor a framework will actually work, but both will create a “walled garden” dynamic, where some companies are put at a competitive advantage at the expense of all the others. Walled gardens can be good orchards for those inside the walls, but excessively tended trees can grow dependent on the gardener, who for their part becomes wont to ignore the Apples outside the walls.

    <p>First, this is not an argument against the state ever intervening to help a particular company. If such idealistic liberalism were ever justified in an internationalised world of open trade, it is not justified now. Instead, this is an argument against picking which companies to support *before they come with specific requests*. There are real problems faced by the companies that seem likely to be important to our economy in future. Government procurement is difficult to work with even for incumbents and it is worse for companies that are scaling up. There is regulatory uncertainty on important issues. There could be more risk capital available in the UK. These problems affect many companies, and the best way to fix them is through systemic change, even if the upside of this is less obviously attributable to government action. But sometimes an individual company will have an issue specific to them that very few other companies face. It would be perverse not to step in with bespoke support if the company is otherwise economically promising. Relying on a case-by-case approach avoids the problems of picking winners in advance, even if it will not earn the plaudits of entrepreneurs and venture capitalists who want promises of future funding.</p> 
    <p>It is an article of faith, in the Treasury as well as in the parts of government that manage investment portfolios (DSIT and UKRI), that decisions are best made in the round. While that is an excellent way to politely sideline unaffordable funding demands, it limits the decision-maker’s ability to take up obviously beneficial options. A VC would never make a list of companies and invest only in those for the coming years; neither should the government.</p>
    <p>Second, the state can legitimately intervene to support companies for non-economic reasons, from security to sovereignty. This is the proper use for relatively small, targeted funds like Sovereign AI’s. They should actively support companies that are important to the state’s delivery of public services or national security. It is far better to use these limited funds to advance the state’s goals – for example, by ensuring that we have the ability to manufacture critical components onshore when that capability exists only in hostile states – than to make a splash investing in a flashy new company that would have succeeded anyway. It is *cool* to appear in Isomorphic Labs’ and Ineffable Intelligence’s cap tables, but it’s not *strategic*. It seems unlikely that new start-ups run by senior figures from DeepMind would struggle to raise in the hottest market in recent history. As the government has taken no golden share, it is unclear how such investment benefits the country. “Strategic national champions” should be national champions picked for strategic reasons, not national champions picked strategically for economic reasons.</p>

Instead of tending a chosen few trees through bespoke, costly, and risky interventions, the government should clear the ground for the whole forest, pulling up as many weeds as it can find and making the soil as fertile as possible. Doing so lets every tree grow more quickly and spares those that would thrive in open soil from being outcompeted by those that get the most attention. Labour market rigidity, regulation, high levels of taxation, and difficulty accessing procurement contracts are weeds that choke every company, and they can be cleared without deciding in advance which plants deserve to flourish.

## Success comes in many forms [[TOC: – Success in many forms]]

A thriving ecosystem of large innovative companies relies on many firms failing to scale. Not all businesses should become incumbents. Moreover, some businesses that don’t even want to do so still produce important economic value that the linear model misses. Since the [1980s](https://sms.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1002/smj.2693), larger companies have become steadily less likely to invest in their own ideas and more likely to acquire smaller innovative companies instead. The small companies do not scale as far as they might, because they are instead bought out by the sort of large innovative companies the government wishes to foster. This important flow of innovation would be throttled if there were perverse incentives for the small companies to scale independently. An acquisition is too often seen as a failure to IPO, when this is not always the case. Scaling should not be prescribed as a panacea. 

Just as not every company needs to IPO to succeed, not every new idea or product needs its own small company. Universities and businesses both recognise this. For example, Oxford has entered into a number of [joint](https://www.ox.ac.uk/news/2024-07-22-apollo-therapeutics-and-oxford-university-enter-drug-discovery-and-development) [research](https://www.ono-pharma.com/en/news/20240322.html) [agreements](https://www.gsk.com/en-gb/media/press-releases/gsk-and-the-university-of-oxford-launch-new-oxford-gsk-institute-to-harness-advanced-technology-and-unravel-mechanisms-of-disease/) with existing life sciences companies. It would be easy to miss the value of licensing and intellectual property agreements by zooming in too far on spin-outs. The UK government, following its linear model, focuses on the [number](https://www.hesa.ac.uk/data-and-analysis/business-community/spin-out-register) of firms spinning out of universities, but is far quieter on these alternative methods of commercialisation. As it faces continued requests for additional proof-of-concept funding to support spin-outs, the government should consider whether there is already undue support for spin-outs over other less legible forms of commercialisation. UKRI’s new £5m [initiative to develop better knowledge exchange metrics](https://www.ukri.org/opportunity/partnership-to-transform-university-knowledge-exchange-metrics/) is welcome evidence that it is beginning to look at this.

The linear model focuses on moving innovation on one step at a time. It therefore deems failure to make the next leap to be a problem to be solved by the state. This ignores the important prioritisation role played by the market, distracts from wider systemic reform, and ignores other innovation flows, from acquisitions to IP transfer. But the linear model’s narrow scope ignores a lot more besides, as we will now see.

## We need to think more broadly than “Growing British tech firms” [[TOC: – Think more broadly]]

The linear model has a very narrow conception of how economic value is created. It over-emphasises the ownership of British, VC-backed, IP-commercialising firms. In doing so, it underplays the importance of other modes of economic value creation. This is leading the government up the garden path, targeting ownership rather than activity, and intervening in ways that enrich shareholders without locking nationally-enriching activity down in the UK.

### Venture capital is not everything

The linear model fetishises companies that take new ideas, especially from universities or high-prestige industries, and scale them through VC funding, with each new funding round a milestone on the journey to eventual listing. While it is true that the very largest businesses in the world have often grown through VC funding, this is no surprise: VC profit models rely on backing the biggest winners to compensate for a large number of losers. In reality, many successful businesses don’t need new scientific ideas and VC funding to have a significant economic impact, offering high-skilled jobs to many people while paying hefty tax bills. 

Scaling businesses need not be centred on brand new research in cutting-edge tech: of the UK’s 41 [unicorns](https://www.beauhurst.com/research/unicorn-companies/), 34 are fintech or SaaS-focused, with several more producing consumer goods. Only 13 offer AI as a product. And venture capital need not be central either: many of the UK’s most successful tech companies, like Dyson, Sage, or Bet365, have grown [without](http://davidconnell.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/07/FINAL-DC001-UK-INNOVATION-AND-INDUSTRIAL-POLICY-3.pdf) VC investment, relying instead on bootstrapping, sweat equity, and revenue from product sales. Despite all this, the government is more interested in deeptech than Deliveroo, AI than fintech.

Some additional emphasis on VC-backed deeptech companies is justified. Left to itself, the private market will [underinvest](https://gemenne.wordpress.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/09/nelson-basic-economics-of-scientific-research.pdf) in R\&D, so there is a stronger case for government intervention. Moreover, since these companies go through a long period of loss-making investment, they rely on investors with patient capital, who are impacted by the state’s pension regulations. But the emphasis on deeptech companies does not justify ignoring the needs of revenue-led firms, which differ in important ways from their new-ideas, VC-backed counterparts. At the moment, public grant funding schemes fund less than 100% of the cost of research, assuming founders will raise capital to cover the gap. This disadvantages revenue-led founders, whose business model doesn’t work this way. Similarly, commercialisation support is routed through universities, so is targeted at founders from academic rather than corporate backgrounds. Compounding this, corporate contracts often lay claim to employees’ IP, putting up another barrier to the start-ups the government claims to want. These are fiddly issues, and much less immediately legible than VC-backed companies’ calls for more equity investment, more PhDs, or more public funding for sectoral research programmes. But revenue-led companies deserve serious consideration and an ambitious innovation strategy would do well to remember this.

### Activity beats paper

The UK will be better able to capture value from companies that stay in the UK. But the linear “start, scale, stay” narrative, as it is currently applied, wrongly assumes that any form of movement overseas is bad. The policy architecture developed over the last decade, from the £500m Sovereign AI Unit to the National Security and Investment Act’s national security provisions, is only coherent if one assumes that foreign capital investment and acquisitions of British companies by foreign companies are negative or second-best outcomes, to be prevented where possible. A House of Lords committee has claimed that the UK is “[bleeding to death](https://publications.parliament.uk/pa/ld5901/ldselect/ldsctech/192/192.pdf)” as its innovative businesses are bought by US capital or competitors. But the [example](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/why-isnt-deepmind-seen-jewel-uk-economic-crown-chris-anderson-gxdic/) of the UK’s highest profile AI company proves that foreign ownership can be a blessing for the UK economy provided that the productive activity stays here. 

When DeepMind was acquired by Google in 2014, it had only around 75 staff and “[a relatively vague description](https://techcrunch.com/2014/01/26/google-deepmind/)”. Now, after billions in R\&D investment from Alphabet, it is one of our most exciting organisations. DeepMind spends more than £1 billion on staff [annually](https://www.theregister.com/software/2025/10/14/brit-ai-boffins-making-bank-with-560k-average-at-anthropic/353333), sits alongside Google at the centre of a powerhouse King’s Cross tech cluster, and has seen its work earn a Nobel Prize. Former DeepMind researchers have [founded](https://www.ft.com/content/8f2a529e-7a1b-4d8e-95be-338d0c4c98f5?syn-25a6b1a6=1) more than a dozen companies since 2021, raising at least $14bn. This should be lauded as a huge success, and one that likely wouldn’t have happened if the business had remained British owned.

Activity, not ownership, is what matters for economic growth. So, the “stay” in “start, scale, stay” must refer to a company’s manufacturing operations, research activity, investment, and employment. These are the factors which determine economic value, stimulate the [local economy](https://eml.berkeley.edu/~moretti/multipliers.pdf), and create virtuous cycles. A company’s ownership, where it lists, and how much it raises matter economically insofar as they affect these primary economic factors. Of course, the location of the board and investors can affect where a company decides to perform its activity. It is not nothing. But the government is acting as if it is almost everything, overindexing on the ownership of companies. It wrongly assumes that Britain’s problems will be solved by just a little more [domestic](https://www.british-business-bank.co.uk/news-and-events/news/british-business-bank-total-financial-capacity-increased-ps255bn) or [European](https://www.ft.com/content/f83bdcd4-c8b2-467a-ae16-c7af45393650?syn-25a6b1a6=1) capital, no matter how slow or bureaucratic.

Of course, there are good reasons not to want our tech companies in hock to investors from hostile states, and national security can offer a compelling case to keep them under British ownership entirely. But any government programmes that aim to keep companies here primarily for economic purposes must be judged by their success in keeping *activity* here, not just paper ownership.

### Keeping activity here means making it attractive to be here

Since what matters is keeping *activity* here, and since ownership isn’t everything, we need to identify how to influence companies’ location decisions. Of course, different companies make decisions in different ways, but it is folly to imagine that most executive boards with fiduciary duties make such decisions based on patriotism. While some take national pride into account, very few executives think like Demis Hassabis – we must not rely on Britmaxxing. Ultimately, businesses think mostly about profit. When they decide where to base their research and manufacturing, they consider what is best for the company in the long run. They do not and should not assume that this is always to stay in the country where they began. Despite all this, the most popular working theory is that companies will stay if only the barriers to doing so – the most important of which is usually seen to be a scale-up capital gap – are removed. The UK is assumed always to be the default. This is not the case. 

Companies will consider where is a good place to do business, looking at stability, energy costs, regulation, tax, market size. They will consider where is a good place to trade from, looking at trade regimes, language, geography. And they will consider where is a good place to access innovation, looking at skilled talent, strong knowledge bases, proximity to other businesses to collaborate with. Ultimately, companies will stay in the UK if it is more attractive in the long-term. The UK offers some major advantages. It has dense technical talent from research-intensive universities, competitive [corporation tax](https://www.oecd.org/en/data/datasets/corporate-income-tax-rates-database.html) rates and allowances, and the English language. But it also has the highest [industrial electricity prices](https://www.gov.uk/government/statistical-data-sets/international-non-domestic-energy-prices) of any country that reports them, a [15-year queue](https://www.gov.uk/government/news/government-to-tackle-speculative-demand-grid-connection-requests) for grid connections, a limited [STEM skills pipeline](https://post.parliament.uk/research-briefings/post-pn-0746/), the highest [up-front costs](https://royalsociety.org/-/media/policy/publications/2025/summary-of-visa-costs-analysis-2025.pdf) for bringing scientists into the country, a much smaller market than the US or EU, and only average [R\&D intensity](https://data-explorer.oecd.org/vis?lc=en&fs[0]=Topic%2C1%7CScience%252C%20technology%20and%20innovation%23INT%23%7CResearch%20and%20development%20%28R%26D%29%23INT_RD%23&pg=0&fc=Topic&bp=true&snb=19&df[ds]=dsDisseminateFinalDMZ&df[id]=DSD_MSTI%40DF_MSTI&df[ag]=OECD.STI.STP&df[vs]=1.3&dq=.A.G%2BT_RS.PT_B1GQ..&to[TIME_PERIOD]=false&pd=2023%2C&vw=tb). If the government wants to keep companies here, it can either improve the environment or compensate for it through firm-specific support. The linear model’s narrow focus on ownership and champions favours the latter approach, even though the former approach benefits all businesses, regardless of the strength of their lobbying efforts. In the long run, the right answer is to focus on the environment.

In the long run, we’re all dead. Companies know that they can bring short-term pressure to secure what they want. Moreover, they know that the government has more control over some things, such as equity, grant and procurement decisions, than others, such as market size. So, companies’ stated preferences will not always be the same as what is best long term either for them or the country. Not everything the government has immediate control of will make a difference to a company’s long-term location, even if companies claim otherwise. Businesses will rarely reject a capital grant, even if it is not what would make them stay in the UK, and many will see public equity funding as a way to secure better terms or simply more liquidity. So, any firm-specific support must be structured to make the UK more attractive than other countries in the future as well as now. Getting this wrong wastes public money on subsidies to companies that would have stayed even without the subsidy or that will go on to leave even with it. Neither is a good way to stimulate growth. Instead, every intervention must change the calculation companies make when deciding where to expand their businesses by making the UK more attractive or making a move away less attractive. Specifically, 

1. **If we provide any grant or equity support, it should by default have explicit clawback provisions.** Unless the government imposes conditional costs on offshoring activity, government funding does little to *ensure* that companies keep their activity here. They will take the money, develop their technology, and then make the rational business decision on whether to move abroad. A big pharma business, with revenues in the tens of billions, is unlikely to base its business strategy on a capital grant of a few million, but has every incentive to say otherwise. A scale-up whose Series B round is supported by government investors has no additional barrier to moving activity abroad later in its development. Clawback provisions increase the costs of moving abroad, while golden shares increase the government’s ability to influence such decisions. Businesses will complain, with good reason, that this makes it harder to grow or raise capital in future. This may well be true, but a British government focused on UK growth should prefer large British benefits over extremely large benefits overseas. If firms do not like this, they are free to reject the bespoke support and call for a better overall UK business environment.

2. **The government should invest in assets that cannot easily be moved abroad.** These assets give the UK an edge by making it relatively more attractive to stay and use the asset than leave and buy the asset afresh. The more specialised, expensive, rooted in a location, and important these assets are, the harder it is for a business to leave them. So, such assets will most often be infrastructure: buildings, deeply interconnected programmes, or expensive bits of kit. Firms will form around them and then, as their business models rely on access, will want to stay. This effect compounds when infrastructure is placed together and a critical mass of talent forms: dense networks of talent can be just as important and hard to move as infrastructure. The technology cluster in Harwell, Oxfordshire is a prime example of this. RAL Space, the UK’s space laboratory, sits at the heart of a [cluster](https://www.harwellcampus.com/space-cluster/) of more than 100 space companies and 1000 jobs. Just a ten-minute walk away are the Vulcan laser, the ISIS Neutron and Muon Source, and the Diamond Light Source synchrotron. Overall, the campus is home to more than 250 organisations, including Oxford Nanopore – one of the few UK spin-outs to trouble the FTSE 250\. Moreover, the businesses actually stay, due to the proximity of infrastructure and talent. Consider Cobalt Light Systems. Cobalt spun out from the Science and Technology Facilities Council in 2008, having used public laser infrastructure to develop techniques for scanning materials through barriers. The tech caught on and is now used around the world in airport scanners. Cobalt was bought in 2017 by Agilent, a major multinational. This looks like a disaster for the start-scale-stay crowd. But nothing could be further from the [truth](https://www.harwellcampus.com/agilents-story/). Agilent has kept Cobalt’s research based in Harwell and has significantly increased investment in the UK. Cobalt started, scaled, was acquired, stayed, and scaled some more, anchored by infrastructure and dense talent networks and supercharged by foreign direct investment. Assets that cannot be moved can have a powerful effect on the likelihood that companies stay, even when they are acquired. This effect, not government subsidy of particular companies, is how Palo Alto became a powerhouse. The government has done good work in deploying the £500m Life Sciences Manufacturing Fund to fund capital assets, and its recent move to fund the new [National Cryogenics Facility](https://www.ukri.org/news/enabling-innovation-at-temperatures-as-cold-as-deep-space/) in Cheshire looks likely to secure more activity from leading quantum scale-ups. But if the government is serious about keeping scale-ups’ activity here, it should be thinking about hard-to-move assets at least as much as scale capital, even if they come up less often in stakeholder discussions[^1].

But if infrastructure and dense networks of talent are good at keeping companies in one place, maybe we should be wary of giving support to companies that are richer in IP than talent or equipment, since they can move away more easily. The UK’s leading talent incubator predominantly creates small software start-ups that are so footloose that they are immediately [shipped over to San Francisco](https://www.joinef.com/posts/introducing-the-bridge/). This is an extremely good way to enrich the talented founders and the owners of the incubator, but it’s probably less good for the UK to send our finest talent across the pond.</p>

The linear model is being applied in a narrow way that privileges individual firms’ requests over the nation’s economic benefit. Firms will rightly ask for more money with no strings attached, as that is what is best for them as companies. They won’t always ask for what will keep them here in the long run: a stable and strong business and trading environment, money with strings attached, or infrastructure that can’t easily be replicated or moved. An economic strategy that prioritised these options would be less popular with firms in the short-run, but better at keeping them here in the long-run.

### An anchor for domestic firms can be a magnet for foreign firms

Many of the things that keep British companies here also attract foreign firms to relocate. Since activity is what matters economically, this can be just as beneficial as growing domestic companies. Just as we want British businesses to “start, scale, and stay”, we need to “court, capture, and keep” foreign firms. The government has begun to recognise this, sending a clear “[message](https://www.independent.co.uk/news/uk/politics/rachel-reeves-chancellor-israel-press-association-b2940462.html) to every entrepreneur looking to build a new AI product: come to the UK.” This is a start, but the framing remains too linear and narrow: we should also seek to attract innovative firms that have started and scaled abroad.

The small Spanish town of San Sebastian – Donostia to the locals – boasts golden beaches, a rich Basque culture, and one of the world’s densest clusters of Michelin stars. It is also home to the [new offices](https://quantummotion.com/quantum-motion-expands-global-presence-by-opening-european-offices-in-spain/) of Quantum Motion, a quantum computing spin-out from Oxford and UCL. Quantum Motion’s CEO put the decision to expand in Spain down to the “state-of-the-art technology infrastructure available in the new Quantum Tower and our collaboration with the Basque government, academia and experts”. Around the same time it expanded into Spain, Quantum Motion raised a $160m [round](https://quantummotion.com/series-c-funding/) co-led by a Spanish VC. Around 250 miles to the south, Nu Quantum, a quantum computing spin-out from Cambridge, has established a subsidiary in Madrid. [As part of the deal](https://www.nu-quantum.com/news/nu-quantum-to-open-a-spanish-subsidiary-as-part-of-government%E2%80%99s-investment), the Spanish government put $11.6m into Nu Quantum’s $60m series A. Spain will benefit from the expansion of two growing British tech companies, without having subsidised their highest-risk research.

This coup is a result of Spain’s 2025 [quantum strategy](https://data.consilium.europa.eu/doc/document/ST-9237-2025-INIT/en/pdf), which provided more than €800m over 5 years for quantum ecosystem development. While it included a linear objective that UK readers would find familiar (“a Spanish quantum market that promotes company creation and access to financing”), it supplements this with a more international objective: “a cohesive national vision that takes advantage of European and international opportunities”. Strategies are just words, however – so it is significant that the Spanish government then used its publicly owned investors intelligently. It made investment clearly conditional on activity moving to Spain, and provided the research infrastructure that made moving attractive. Critically, Spain has pursued these deals with non-Spanish firms, seeking economic benefit beyond its borders. The British government should be similarly open across all the technologies it is targeting: targeting whichever firms might increase their economic activity in the UK, whether or not they are British. To do this effectively, it should beef up the Science and Technology Network and the Office for Investment so that the state can seriously consider the opportunities to partner with foreign firms. But convincing firms to move here is much easier if it is already very attractive to operate in the UK, and we should consider unorthodox approaches to make this a reality. Paradoxically, limiting competitions to British firms can increase the attractiveness of the UK to international firms. For example, in the months after the government announced that it would spend up to £1 billion on British commercial-scale quantum computers, Rigetti [announced](https://investors.rigetti.com/news-releases/news-release-details/rigetti-computing-intends-invest-100-million-uk-accelerate) its intention to invest $100 million into the UK and Infleqtion [announced](https://thequantuminsider.com/2026/05/28/infleqtion-expands-uk-quantum-operations-with-new-oxford-innovation-centre-and-manufacturing-hub/) a major expansion of its Oxford-based quantum innovation and manufacturing facilities. The recent AI hardware plan could well bear similar fruit. Foreign firms will want to become British firms if there are clear benefits associated with that status.

## Section C: Why the Model Persists [[TOC: Why The Model Persists]]

The linear model offers only a brittle conceptualisation of innovation. It cannot account for how ideas and technologies are produced in reality, or for the fact that some ideas and technologies are much more important than others. The state fears firm failure, has delusions about its ability to identify winners in advance, underplays important ways of getting growth from innovation, and focuses far too narrowly on a subset of British VC-backed tech firms, all the while assuming that the solution is to give its favoured firms more money. A better strategy would have a broader focus, embracing international, revenue-led firms, and firms outside deeptech sectors, through ambitious action to improve the business and trade environment, while providing bespoke support on a case-by-case basis. Why then does the linear model dominate, despite all its drawbacks? 

First, linear is legible. Innovation is a broad, nebulous term, covering many different processes across complex systems. Each of these processes has a high barrier to entry and may seem intimidating. How many policymakers and commentators can differentiate gene splicing from genome sequencing? Of those, how many can explain when we should rely on pre-training rather than post-training or fine-tuning, or what the most cost effective way to test a new prototype is? Does the rare person who can do all this have experience raising rounds, navigating a cap table, or running an investment portfolio? Even if the policymaker is almost comically well suited to the job,[^2] they will be faced with a jungle of choices and expected to map out the way forward to others less experienced than themselves. It would take anyone at least a few minutes to explain cycles of disruptive invention to a cabinet minister, and cabinet ministers rarely have a few minutes to spare. The linear model solves all these problems. It abstracts away from the dense detail and is simple enough that it can be explained in seconds to a non-expert. 

Second, the linear model is politically convenient. Crucially, its idea of success is non-controversial. All but the fringe agree that having many large high-tech companies offering high-wage jobs would be a good thing. It also provides compelling stories on the way to success. Entrepreneurs can become heroes: just ask Demis. The compelling success stories are trumpeted by those with the most to gain from the linear model: the incumbents in the UK’s innovation ecosystem. Research-intensive universities position themselves as engines for growth and call for [more funding](https://www.russellgroup.ac.uk/sites/default/files/2025-03/Russell%20Group%20Spending%20Review%202025%20full%20submission.pdf) for the “pipeline of new ideas”, while venture capitalists and entrepreneurs call for ever more scale capital – the co-founder of ARM and Amadeus recently told SXSW that the €5 billion EU Scale Up fund “is not going to be enough” and that it should be “maybe eventually €500 billion”. When such impressive and important innovators are speaking in linear terms, it is politically easiest to do the same. 

Finally, the linear model survives because it offers politicians a chance to have visible and attributable impact over a single parliamentary term. Ministers and senior officials often justifiably want a cabinet position or a permanent secretary role. They almost always want at least to remain in their job. Having these desires is the key de facto selection criterion for their position: they wouldn’t have reached their position without them. The path upwards requires visible and attributable success: achievements and announcements that can be presented as a direct result of their actions. Even if the most effective approach is one that requires long-term investment in underpinning infrastructure, the benefits of that might be twenty years away, and will in any case seem to be the result of a genius entrepreneur. Of course, decision-makers want what is good for the country – but the options that they consider and pitch into the wider policymaking machine are coloured by the need to show their success, not be accused of failures that seem avoidable, and to do all of this over a few short years. Consider again the choice at the start of the essay. How much easier it would be to try to push ideas and firms through the pipeline as quickly as possible. How much better it would be not to.
