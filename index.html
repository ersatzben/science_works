Science Works is an independent research and policy studio aiming to tackle the biggest problems facing UK science and technology.

Explore Our Work About Us

Britain needs a metascientific revolution.

The UK's place in the world has long been underwritten by the creativity of its laboratories, workshops, and universities. Today that imagination is running into the limits of institutions built for another era. We've lost much of the freedom that once defined British science, and failed to seize the possibilities of our technological age.

We have deep talent, trusted universities, and a culture of rigour. But the systems that fund and govern research move slowly, tangled in crude metrics and misaligned incentives, while public science lags behind the private frontier.

Science Works exists to change that.

Institutional Dynamism

The UK’s research ecosystem is unusually homogeneous and slow to evolve

In other countries, R&D is spread across a much wider range of institutional forms. We want to inject diversity and dynamism into the sector – mapping the institutional models available to funders and policymakers, strengthening the state’s capacity to assess and support proposals for new organisations, and incubating new organisational forms where we are best placed to do so.

AI and Science

Artificial intelligence is poised to change what it means to be a scientist

But the UK risks compounding existing dysfunctions rather than unlocking the gains: much of the scientific record remains invisible, research software is undervalued, and the infrastructure for AI-enabled discovery is unevenly available. We are developing an ambitious British agenda for AI in science – covering data, software, automation, and the institutional changes needed to make them count.

The Research Economy

The UK funds research without a clear picture of its real costs and trade-offs

We bring quantitative and qualitative analysis to bear on the research base: highlighting the hidden costs, frictions, trade-offs, and cross-subsidies that underpin the economics of institutions and the architecture of national R&D spending. We pair this with detailed mapping of the system’s structure, and with practical work on the design of funding and assessment, pushing for a system that is less bureaucratic, more honest, and more dynamic.

Software for Science

The future of research is digital—and we need to adapt

Scientists lack modern and interoperable software tools for undertaking research in the age of intelligence. The research software engineer is a forgotten role without a clear career path. Much of the research process remains analogue; there is a lack of a well-functioning market for software; open tools are poorly maintained. We are building and testing new software tools to address this gap, while working on the incentives that will allow software to flourish.

The Story of British Science

Scientific progress requires public confidence and political willpower

Neither is guaranteed to endure over the coming decade. The UK currently lacks a compelling, forward-looking story about what its scientific and technological future could be. We want to help develop and articulate an account of British scientific renewal that can resonate across the political spectrum.
